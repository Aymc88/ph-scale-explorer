1 What is pH?

pH stands for "power of Hydrogen." It tells us how acidic or basic a water-based solution is, on a scale from 0 to 14.

In plain words

Think of pH as a thermometer for acidity. Below 7 is acidic (like lemon juice). Exactly 7 is neutral (like pure water). Above 7 is basic (like baking soda).

Example: Coffee has a pH of about 5 — slightly acidic. Soap has a pH of about 10 — basic.

Why does it matter?

Each unit on the pH scale is 10× stronger than the next. A drink at pH 3 is 100× more acidic than one at pH 5. That's why even small pH differences in your stomach, blood, or pool can have big effects.

Example: Stomach acid (pH ~1.5) is about a million times more acidic than blood (pH ~7.4).

3 The Ions Behind pH: H⁺ and OH⁻

pH measures the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺) in a solution. More H⁺ → more acidic. More OH⁻ → more basic.

H⁺: 10  ·  OH⁻: 10  ·  pH ≈ 7.0

The chemistry

In water, a tiny fraction of H₂O molecules split into H⁺ and OH⁻ ions. Adding an acid (like HCl) increases H⁺. Adding a base (like NaOH) increases OH⁻.

pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]

Example: If [H⁺] = 0.0001 mol/L = 10⁻⁴ mol/L, then pH = 4.

4 Acids: Sour, Reactive, and Useful

Acids have a pH below 7. They taste sour, can corrode metals, and release H⁺ ions in water.

Properties of Acids

  • Sour taste (citrus, vinegar)
  • Turn blue litmus paper red
  • React with metals to release H₂ gas
  • Conduct electricity (electrolytes)
  • Donate H⁺ ions in water

Common Acids

HCl — stomach acid · H₂SO₄ — car batteries · CH₃COOH — vinegar · C₆H₈O₇ — lemons

5 Bases: Bitter, Slippery, and Powerful

Bases have a pH above 7. They feel slippery, taste bitter, and release OH⁻ ions in water.

Properties of Bases

  • Bitter taste, slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus paper blue
  • Dissolve oils and fats (cleaning!)
  • Conduct electricity in solution
  • Donate OH⁻ ions in water

Common Bases

NaOH — drain cleaner · NaHCO₃ — baking soda · NH₃ — ammonia · Mg(OH)₂ — antacids

6 Neutral: The Special Case of Pure Water

Pure water has a pH of exactly 7.0. Its H⁺ and OH⁻ are perfectly balanced.

The balance

In pure water at 25 °C, [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ mol/L. The product [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ — this is the ion product of water, Kw.

Example: Rainwater isn't truly pH 7 — it absorbs CO₂ from the air and becomes slightly acidic, around pH 5.6.

7 pH Indicators Change Color

Indicators are special chemicals that change color depending on the pH of a solution. Pick an indicator and a pH below.

How indicators work

Indicators are weak acids or bases whose own molecules have one color and whose ions have another. The color you see depends on which form dominates at a given pH.

Example: Phenolphthalein is colorless below pH 8.2 and bright pink above pH 10 — it's the classic indicator for titration labs.

8 Neutralization: When Acid Meets Base

When an acid and a base mix, they cancel each other out. The product is salt + water.

The reaction in words

H⁺ ions from the acid and OH⁻ ions from the base combine to form water. The remaining ions form a salt.

Example: If you have heartburn, an antacid (Mg(OH)₂) neutralizes the excess stomach acid (HCl) — the same chemistry, inside your body!

9 pH in Everyday Life

pH controls how our bodies, our food, and our environment work.

🩸

Blood

pH 7.35 – 7.45

Tightly controlled — even a small change can be dangerous.

🌱

Soil

pH 6.0 – 7.5

Most plants prefer slightly acidic soil. Blueberries love pH ~4.5!

🌊

Ocean

pH ~8.1

Slightly basic. CO₂ is making it more acidic — "ocean acidification."

🦷

Mouth

pH ~6.7 – 7.3

Below pH 5.5, tooth enamel begins to dissolve. Soda is pH ~2.5!

Why this matters

Living systems are sensitive to pH. Enzymes, the molecules that run our biology, only work in narrow pH ranges. That's why your body uses "buffers" to keep blood pH within ±0.05.

10 How Do We Measure pH?

Three common ways scientists measure pH:

1. Indicator Paper

Cheap, fast strip. Compare the color to a chart.

Accuracy: ±0.5 pH

2. Digital pH Meter

Electrode measures voltage; reads pH directly.

Accuracy: ±0.01 pH

3. Universal Indicator

Drop the liquid into the sample and read the color.

Accuracy: ±0.5 pH

Try it yourself

You can make a natural indicator from red cabbage juice! It turns pink in acid, purple in neutral, and green in base. Boil chopped cabbage in water, strain, and use the purple liquid to test household items like vinegar, soap, and lemon juice.

Lab safety: Wear goggles, never taste-test, and rinse thoroughly with water if any acid or base touches your skin.
